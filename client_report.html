Client / Developer Team Report

A reflection on my experience in each role during the WDD 130 team project.

Client Report

As the client, I was responsible for creating the site plan for my personal website and communicating my vision to my development team.

How did you decide what content and images to use for your site plan?

I started by thinking about what I wanted the site to represent — my background, interests, and goals as a student. I decided to keep the content personal but professional, focusing on my tech coursework and my interest in hands-on projects. For the color palette I used Coolors.co to build a scheme around a deep navy blue and amber, which felt clean and confident without being overdone. For images I looked for ones that felt authentic rather than generic stock photos, and I made sure anything I chose was free to use.

How did you communicate with your developer? Were there any issues with communication?

I communicated with my lead developer primarily through class time and text messages. I shared my completed site plan early so they had a clear reference for colors, fonts, and layout expectations. There were a couple of small misunderstandings about which images to use in certain sections, but we sorted those out quickly by going back to the site plan together. Overall communication was smooth and I felt like my lead developer genuinely tried to follow the plan.

Did your developer meet your expectations for your site?

Yes, for the most part. The home page came out close to what I had envisioned from the wireframe. The nav, color scheme, and overall structure matched the site plan well. There were a few minor differences in spacing and font sizing, but nothing that broke the design. I appreciated that my developer asked questions rather than just guessing when something was unclear.

What will you do differently next time you hire a team to do a project for you?

I would put more detail into the wireframe upfront. I kept mine fairly simple, and a few times my developer had to make judgment calls on layout that I then wanted adjusted. More detailed wireframes for each section — including approximate spacing and which elements sit next to each other — would have saved some back and forth. I would also set a midpoint check-in deadline so I could see progress before the final submission.

Lead Developer Report

As the lead developer, I was responsible for building my client's home page, managing the GitHub repository, publishing the site, and coordinating with a junior developer.

How did you manage your development team and communicate the tasks to your junior dev?

I started by reviewing the client's site plan thoroughly before writing any code so I had a clear picture of the full site. Once I understood the structure, I communicated with my junior developer to explain which pages they were responsible for and what classes and IDs to use so our code would stay consistent. I shared the GitHub repository link and walked them through cloning it. We checked in briefly each class period to make sure things were on track.

How did you communicate with your client? Were there any issues?

I touched base with my client after completing the initial HTML structure and again after adding CSS. I shared the GitHub Pages link both times so they could preview the live site. There was one point where the client wanted the hero section to look different from what the wireframe showed, which required a conversation about what was realistic given our timeline. We landed on a compromise that satisfied both of us. Outside of that, communication was straightforward.

How did you handle publishing your client's site and sharing it with the client?

I set up the GitHub repository early and enabled GitHub Pages from the main branch right away so there was always a live preview available. I shared the URL with my client in a text message and also wrote it on a sticky note during class. Keeping the published link active throughout the project made it easy to show progress without having to send files back and forth.

How was your experience with managing your site with GitHub?

It went well overall. I got comfortable with the basic workflow — editing files in VS Code, staging changes, committing, and pushing to the remote repository. The trickiest part was when my junior developer pushed a change that conflicted with something I had already committed. We had to walk through resolving the merge conflict together, which was frustrating at first but actually a really valuable learning experience. By the end of the project I felt noticeably more confident with Git than I did at the start.

Junior Developer Report

As a junior developer, I was a member of another student's development team, responsible for building the two child pages of their site.

How did you communicate with your team lead? Were there any issues?

My team lead was organized and upfront about expectations from the beginning, which made communication easy. We talked during class and occasionally texted when something came up between sessions. There was one point where I was unclear on the exact file naming convention the lead had set up, which caused a brief issue when I pushed my files to the repository. Once I clarified that with the lead it was a quick fix. No major issues overall.

Did you know what was expected of you for the site?

Yes. My lead developer shared the client's site plan with me early so I understood the color palette, fonts, and general style. I was told which two pages to build and given the existing stylesheet to work from so the child pages would stay visually consistent with the home page. Having that context upfront made a big difference — I wasn't guessing about design decisions, I could just focus on building the pages correctly.

Were you able to contribute to your site?

Yes. I completed both child pages on time and followed the design system established by the lead developer. I made sure to use the same CSS file, match the header and footer structure exactly, and use the same class names that were already in place. I also added a small hover effect on the navigation links that the lead had not gotten to yet, which the client appreciated.

Did you run into any issues with GitHub and adding your changes to the site?

The merge conflict I mentioned earlier was the main hurdle. I had edited a CSS file at the same time as my lead, and when I tried to push my changes GitHub flagged the conflict. I had not dealt with a merge conflict before so it was a little stressful in the moment. My lead helped me understand what the conflict markers meant and how to resolve them manually. After that I made sure to always pull the latest changes before starting a new work session, which prevented any further issues.

General Experience and Reflections

What was the most challenging part of this project for you? Were you able to overcome it?

The most challenging part was managing the overlap between all three roles simultaneously. At any given point I was thinking about my own site as a client, actively coding someone else's site as a developer, and contributing pages on a third site as a junior dev. Keeping track of which repository I was working in and which stylesheets belonged to which project required more organization than I expected. I dealt with it by keeping a simple notes document with each project's repo link, my role, and what I still needed to do. That helped a lot.

What did you enjoy most about this project?

I genuinely enjoyed the collaborative aspect. Most of my coding up to this point had been solo work, and working with a team — even a small one — changed how I thought about writing code. Knowing that another person was going to read, use, and build on my code made me much more careful about naming things clearly, keeping my CSS organized, and writing consistent structure. I also found it satisfying to see three separate sites come together across three different teams.

What would you do differently if you could do the project again?

I would establish a shared communication channel — maybe a group chat that included all team members — rather than relying on separate one-on-one conversations. There were a couple of times where information got passed along incompletely because it had to travel through multiple people. A single place where everyone could see updates would have made coordination smoother. I would also spend more time on the site plan phase before any code was written, since vague wireframes created more revision work later.

Do you feel more confident in working on real-world projects after this experience? Explain.

Definitely. Before this project, my understanding of web development was almost entirely individual — write code, check the browser, repeat. This project introduced the parts that actually matter in professional work: version control with multiple contributors, communicating design intent across a team, handling merge conflicts, and publishing a live site for a client to review. Those are real skills. I feel like I have a much better mental model of how web projects actually get built, and that makes me more confident going into more advanced coursework.
